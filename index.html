Julius Caesar's Rise to Power

Julius Caesar, a well-known figure in Roman history, rose to power through his tactical ability and political keenness. In 59 BCE, Caesar got his first consulship, Which was the start of his groundbreaking effect on Roman Politics. During his consulship, he shaped a union with Pompey and Crassus, known as the First Triumvirate, which allowed them to fortify their power and persue after their interests. The alliance, on the other hand, eventually broke down, triggering a power struggle in Rome.

One of Caesar's most significant contributions to Roman history was his leadership in the Gallic Wars (58-50 BCE). He began a series of military campaigns to expand Roman territory. Caesar's triumph of Gaul showed his brilliance in strategy and cemented his standing as a powerful and intelligent military leader. His Commentarii de Bello Gallico, a firsthand record of the conflicts, stays a significant verifiable source.

As Caesar's power and influence grew, tensions rose between him and the Senate, which lead to a series of events that prompted the Roman Civil War (49-45 BCE). Caesar's crossing of the Rubicon river in 49 BCE marked the beginning of the conflict. The war set Caesar in opposition to the powers of the Senate, drove by Pompey, his previous ally. The Battle of Pharsalus in 48 BCE was the eventual turning point in the war, with Caesar emerging victorious and resulting in Pompey fleeing to Egypt, where he died not long after, marking a turning point in history. The Civil War at last brought about Caesar's ascent to outright power and the finish of the Roman Republic. Caesar's crossing of the Rubicon river and the resulting Civil War are significant moments that forever changed the Roman Republic and history as we know it.


The Fall of Julius Caesar and the start of the Roman Empire

The death of Julius Caesar on the Ides of Spring (Walk 15) in 44 BCE is quite possibly of the most famous event in Roman history. Caesar had gathered remarkable power, proclaimed himself as tyrant claiming that only he shall rule, and confronted developing resistance from legislators who dreaded his rising power. A gathering of backstabbers, including notable figures like Cassius and Brutus, coordinated a plot to wipe out Caesar and re-establish the Roman Republic. Upon the arrival of the death, as Caesar entered the Senate meeting at the Theater of Pompey, he was encircled by the schemers who went after him with knifes. As indicated by verifiable records, even Brutus, whom Caesar thought was a close ally, was among the individuals who struck him. Caesar received the fatal blow from Brutus, and he died from his wounds with the well-known last words, "Et tu, Brute?" showing the shock of betrayal he felt.

The death of Julius Caesar had many consequences for the Roman Republic. Instead of re-establishing the traditional republican system, the assassination placed Rome into a time of political turmoil and civil unrest. It set the stage for a series of conflicts that in the end prompted the ascent of Caesar's grandnephew and adopted son, Octavian (later known as Augustus), and the foundation of the Roman Empire. The assassination of Julius Caesar is known as a turning point in Roman history, signalling the end of the Roman Republic and the beginning of the new Roman Empire.

Following the death of Cornelia, Caesar marries Pompeia, a distant relative of Pompey the Great, further solidifying his political connections.

65 BCE
60 BCE

Formation of the First Triumvirate, an alliance between Caesar, Pompey, and Crassus, to consolidate power and pursue their political agendas.

Caesar becomes consul, marking a significant step in his political career. During his consulship, he passes laws that benefit both himself and his allies.

59 BCE
58 - 50 BCE

Caesar leads the Gallic Wars, successfully conquering Gaul and expanding Roman territory. He writes Commentarii de Bello Gallico to document his campaigns.

Crossing the Rubicon River, Caesar defies the Senate and enters Italy, initiating the Roman Civil War against Pompey and the Senate.

49 BCE
48 BCE

Caesar defeats Pompey at the Battle of Pharsalus, leading to Pompey's death in Egypt.

After further victories in Egypt and North Africa, Caesar returns to Rome, consolidating power and implementing various reforms.

45 BCE
44 BCE

Caesar is declared dictator. On the Ides of March (March 15), he is assassinated by a group of senators, including Brutus and Cassius.

The power struggle following Caesar's death leads to the formation of the Second Triumvirate, consisting of Octavian (Caesar's adopted son), Mark Antony, and Marcus Lepidus.

43 BCE
42 BCE

The Battle of Philippi results in the defeat of the forces led by Brutus and Cassius, the principal conspirators in Caesar's assassination.

The Battle of Actium takes place, marking the beginning of the end for the Second Triumvirate. Octavian defeats Antony and Cleopatra, solidifying his position as the sole ruler of Rome.

31 BCE
27 BCE

Octavian, now known as Augustus, becomes the first Roman Emperor, marking the end of the Roman Republic and the beginning of the Roman Empire.
